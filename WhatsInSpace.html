What Are Satellites, How Many Are There, and What Purpose Do They Serve?

What Are Satellites?

Technically speaking, a satellite is a moon, planet or machine that orbits a planet or star. However, the term usually refers to a piece of technology launched into space that revolves around Earth.

Thousands of such man-mad, or "artificial" satellites are currently in orbit, serving us in numerous ways - taking pictures, predicting weather patterns, assisting communications such as television or phone calls, and even for location services, like GPS.

Why Are Satellites Important?

A satellite's altitude and perspective allows them to see vast amounts of the Earth at one time. This means that they can collect much more data, much more quickly, than we can on the ground.

A satellite's altitude also allows them to see better into space than regular telescopes on the Earth's surfact because they fly above clouds, dust, and other molecules in the Earth's atmosphere than can often block our view from the ground.

Before satellites, TV signals could not travel very far because they travel in straight lines and quickly travel off into space rather than following the Earth's curvature. Additionally, they can be blocked by mountains or tall buildings. Phone calls to faraway places also faced similar problems, for example, setting up telephone wires over long distances or underwater is very difficult and expensive.

With satellites, however, TV signals and phone calls can be sent upward to a satellite. Then, almost instantly, the satellite can reflect the signal to send them back down to different locations on Earth without such interference.

What Are the Parts of a Satellite?

Satellites are built in many shapes and sizes depending on their function, but have at least two important parts in common - an antenna and a power source. The antenna is what allows the satellite to send and receive information, often to and from Earth. The power source can be a solar panel or battery. Solar panels make power by turning sunlight into electricity.

Many satellites also carry cameras and scientific sensors. Sometimes these instruments point toward Earth to gather information about its land, air and water. Other times they face toward space to collect data from the solar system and universe.

How Do Satellites Orbit Earth?

Satellites are launched into space on rockets, and begin to orbit the Earth when their speed becomes balanced with the pull of Earth's gravity. Without this equilibrium, the satellite would fly in a straight line off into space or fall back to Earth. Satellites can orbit Earth at different heights, different speeds and along different paths. The two most common types of orbit are "geostationary" and "polar."

A geostationary satellite travels from west to east over the equator. It moves in the same direction and at the same rate Earth is spinning. From Earth, a geostationary satellite looks like it is standing still since it is always above the same location.

Polar-orbiting satellites travel in a north-south direction from pole to pole. As Earth spins underneath, these satellites can scan the entire globe, one strip at a time.

Why Don't Satellites Crash Into Each Other?

Actually, they can. NASA and other U.S. and international organizations keep track of satellites in space. Collisions are rare because when a satellite is launched, it is placed into an orbit designed to avoid other satellites. But orbits can change over time. And the chances of a crash increase as more and more satellites are launched into space.

In February 2009, two communications satellites - one American and one Russian - collided in space. This, however, is believed to be the first time two man-made satellites have collided accidentally.

How Does NASA Use Satellites Today?

Satellites help scientists study Earth and space.

Satellites looking toward Earth provide information about clouds, oceans, land and ice. They also measure gases in the atmosphere, such as ozone and carbon dioxide, and the amount of energy that Earth absorbs and emits. And satellites monitor wildfires, volcanoes and their smoke.

All this information helps scientists predict weather and climate. The information also helps public health officials track disease and famine; it helps farmers know what crops to plant; and it helps emergency workers respond to natural disasters.

Satellites that face toward space have a variety of jobs. Some watch for dangerous rays coming from the sun. Others explore asteroids and comets, the history of stars, and the origin of planets. Some satellites fly near or orbit other planets. These spacecraft may look for evidence of water on Mars or capture close-up pictures of Saturn's rings.
